Overview

Redefining Health: A Resistive, Omnidirectional Surface for Gamified Fitness

Traditional treadmills only support unidirectional movement, limiting their utility across rehabilitation, VR gaming, military training, and education. This MEng capstone project, developed in partnership with Blue Goji®, designed and built a fully passive omnidirectional treadmill that allows a user to walk in any direction — returning them to centre through gravity — without any motors driving the surface.

The system is comprised of a centre base plate and eight angled side plates, each housing freely rotating balls enclosed between two plates with spherical cutouts. The side plates are angled at 15°, using gravity to passively return the user to the neutral centre position. Two distinct braking systems control resistance — electromagnets on the centre plate and friction actuators on the side plates — reducing BOM cost by 50% compared to all-electromagnetic alternatives.

Design

CAD Model & Structural Validation

The full assembly was modelled in SolidWorks. The octagonal platform integrates the centre electromagnetic plate, eight friction-braked angled plates, and the structural aluminium extrusion frame beneath. FEA was performed to validate structural integrity under human body weight loading — confirming stress concentrations remain within safe limits at the centre plate junction.

Engineering

Dual Braking System Architecture

The key innovation of this design is the hybrid braking strategy — using electromagnets on the high-priority centre plate for precise variable resistance, and a simpler rubber friction system on the side plates where cost-efficiency matters more. This split approach is what enabled the 50% BOM reduction while maintaining full-platform control.

Centre Plate — Electromagnetic
Magnet Braking System

A bed of electromagnets beneath the centre plate controls ball motion at variable rates depending on current applied. This provides precise, programmable resistance — allowing the system to simulate different terrain types and difficulty levels. The design also achieves a shorter centre profile and provides greater structural support.

Side Plates — Friction
Friction Braking System

The eight angled side plates use a rubber friction material applied when a motor-driven platform rises and lowers. While less precise than the electromagnetic system, this approach is cost-effective and efficient — since users spend the majority of active movement time on the centre plate, high-precision resistance at the periphery is unnecessary.

Build & Demo

Physical Prototype

The team fabricated and assembled a full working prototype, presented at the UC Berkeley MEng Capstone Demo Day. The build involved CNC machining of the ball housing plates, 3D printing of custom components, and assembly of the aluminium extrusion frame with the electromagnetic and motor actuation systems.
