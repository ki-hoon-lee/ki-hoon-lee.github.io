Overview

Bio-Inspired Adhesion Without the Cost of CVD

Geckos can walk upside down thanks to millions of microscopic hair-like structures called setae, which branch into even finer spatulae — maximising contact area and generating strong van der Waals adhesion without any liquid or residue. Replicating this in synthetic materials is a major challenge in nanotechnology.

This project proposes a low-cost, scalable alternative to expensive Chemical Vapour Deposition (CVD) techniques by magnetically aligning multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) embedded with Fe₃O₄ magnetic nanoparticles within a flexible adhesive substrate. Simulation of the alignment process and JKR adhesion modelling demonstrate the viability of this approach — achieving a theoretical peak pull-off pressure of 471,000 N/m² and a 2.3× improvement over randomly oriented CNTs.

Problem Space

Bridging Two Inadequate Approaches

Existing synthetic gecko adhesives sit at two unsatisfactory extremes. This project targets the gap between them.

Approach A — Too Expensive
CVD-Grown VACNTs

Vertically aligned CNTs grown via Chemical Vapour Deposition achieve strong adhesion but require vacuum systems, 500–1000°C furnace temperatures, rigid substrates (e.g. PMMA), and are not scalable for large-area production.

Approach B — Too Weak
Randomly Applied CNTs

Depositing CNTs directly onto flexible substrates avoids CVD costs but results in poor directional control — misaligned tips create minimal contact area, severely reducing adhesion force and making them impractical as dry adhesives.

This Project — The Bridge
Magnetically Aligned MWCNTs

By embedding Fe₃O₄ nanoparticles into CNTs and applying a neodymium magnet array during ambient-temperature curing, partial vertical alignment is achieved on flexible PDMS/nitrile substrates — combining low cost with meaningful directional control.

Design & Fabrication

5-Step Ambient-Condition Pipeline

The entire fabrication process runs at room temperature and atmospheric pressure — no vacuum, no furnace. The key materials are MWCNTs mixed with Fe₃O₄ nanoparticles in a 10:1 mass ratio in IPA, deposited onto a flexible PDMS or nitrile rubber substrate, and cured under a static neodymium magnet array.

STEP 01
Suspension

MWCNTs + Fe₃O₄ mixed 10:1 in IPA, sonicated for uniform dispersion and CNT debundling.

STEP 02
Substrate Prep

Thin adhesive layer spray-coated onto flexible PDMS or nitrile rubber substrate.

STEP 03
Deposition

CNT-nanoparticle suspension drop-cast or sprayed onto the adhesive layer.

STEP 04
Magnetic Alignment

Substrate placed above neodymium magnet array during ~1 hour curing. Magnetic torque rotates CNTs toward vertical.

STEP 05
Curing

Solvent evaporates, adhesive solidifies, locking CNTs in their aligned orientation permanently.

Simulation & Modelling

Two-Stage Computational Analysis

Stage 1 — Magnetic Alignment Dynamics

The rotational behaviour of 1,000 CNTs (each initialised at a random angle θ₀ from 0°–90°) was modelled as a first-order ODE governed by the balance between magnetic torque and viscous drag:

Magnetic Torque Equation
τ = μB sin θ  →  dθ/dt = −(μB/ζ) sin θ
τ — magnetic torque (N·m)
μ — magnetic moment of CNT-nanoparticle composite (A·m²)
B — external magnetic field strength (T)
θ — angle between CNT and vertical axis (rad)
ζ — rotational drag coefficient (N·m·s/rad)

Simulations across field strengths from 0.01T to 0.3T showed a sharp transition from broad angular distributions to tightly clustered peaks near θ=0° at higher fields, confirming effective alignment. Alignment sharpness was quantified as the mean cos²θ across the population — approaching 1.0 at B=2T in extended simulations.

Stage 2 — Adhesion Force via JKR Theory

The Johnson-Kendall-Roberts contact mechanics model was used to link alignment quality to pull-off force. Non-vertical CNTs contribute less adhesion — their effective force scales with cos²θ:

JKR Pull-Off Force
F₀ = (3/2)πRW cos θ     F_total = N₀ F₀ cos²θ
R — effective CNT tip contact radius (20 nm)
W — work of adhesion (50 mJ/m², van der Waals dry contact)
N₀ — CNT areal density (10¹¹ CNTs/m², consistent with VACNT literature)
Results

Key Outcomes

Peak Pull-Off Pressure
471,000 N/m²

Calculated at ideal vertical alignment (cos²θ = 1) using literature-validated parameters: 20nm tip radius, 50 mJ/m² work of adhesion, 10¹¹ CNTs/m² density. Sufficient to support a 70kg person with just 1.46 cm² of contact area.

Adhesion Improvement
2.3× vs Random

Magnetically aligned CNTs demonstrated a 2.3× increase in estimated pull-off force compared to randomly oriented CNTs — confirming that even partial alignment significantly improves adhesive performance through directional nanoscale contact.

Alignment Sharpness
Nonlinear with B

Mean cos²θ increased nonlinearly with field strength — rapidly improving at low fields, then plateauing due to viscous drag and aggregation. At 0.3T, significant clustering near θ=0° was confirmed across the simulated CNT population.

Fabrication Advantage
Ambient Conditions

No vacuum or high-temperature processing required. Tunable via field strength. Compatible with flexible substrates (PDMS, nitrile). Scalable for large-area production — enabling roll-to-roll manufacturing for soft robotics and wearable applications.
