Overview

How Do Different Flight Strategies Optimise Range for a Solar-Powered UAV?

This final year BEng project was conducted in collaboration with Limosaero, a Cambridge start-up developing a sub-20kg Low Altitude Long Endurance (LALE) solar UAV for applications including wildfire detection, rare earth exploration, and atmospheric monitoring. Unlike high-altitude platforms that operate above cloud cover, Limosaero's UAV must contend with variable cloud cover at 2,000m altitude — making flight strategy critical to range and endurance.

The project developed a 2-degree-of-freedom Python flight simulator capable of modelling how variations in UAV speed and trajectory — combined with real-world BBC Weather cloud imagery and solar irradiance calculations — affect battery level and range across summer solstice, equinox, and winter solstice conditions.

Method

Three-Module Python Simulation

The simulator was built across three interconnected Python modules, each solving a distinct part of the problem before feeding into a unified flight simulation engine using Pygame for real-time 2D visualisation.

MODULE 01
Solar Irradiance Calculator

Takes user inputs (latitude, date, year) and computes declination angle, solar noon, sunrise, and sunset times. Outputs a 1,440-point solar irradiance profile — one per minute of the day — peaking at up to 1,161 W/m² at summer solstice.

MODULE 02
Cloud Image Processor

Processes 512×512px BBC Weather cloud satellite images using OpenCV and NumPy. Each pixel is mapped to a cloud intensity coefficient (1.0 = clear, 0.30 = maximum overcast) to produce a spatial grid of solar transmittance values.

MODULE 03
Flight Simulator

A Pygame-based 2-DOF simulator moves a UAV point source over the cloud grid. At each minute tick, power in (solar × cloud coefficient × panel efficiency × area) and power out (friction + induced drag × velocity) are calculated, updating the battery level in real time.

Results

Optimal Strategy by Season

Simulations were run at latitude 44.5° (UK average) across summer solstice (Jun-20), equinox (Mar-20), and winter solstice (Dec-21), testing six speed configurations from 5–30 m/s. Final battery level upon completing a 65km mission was the primary performance metric. Trajectory modification was tested against five real BBC cloud images using 30 pairs of straight vs. modified path simulations.

88.69 Wh

Peak irradiance only 265 W/m². No perpetual flight possible. Fly at minimum drag speed (12 m/s) at all times. Modified trajectory recommended 9am–2pm to maximise cloud avoidance and extend flight duration.

Key Findings

What the Simulations Revealed

Minimum drag speed (12 m/s) is optimal under zero sunlight — at this speed, the balance between friction and induced drag is minimised, conserving battery. As solar power increases, higher speeds become viable since the surplus energy offsets the greater drag penalty.

Cloud avoidance only pays off under the right conditions. Detouring around clouds adds distance and therefore power consumption. The simulation quantified the crossover point: trajectory modification is beneficial only when solar irradiance is high enough that the additional solar capture along the modified path outweighs the power cost of the extra distance flown.

Sunset is more punishing than sunrise for modified trajectories. When the sun is rising, extra flight time continues to accrue solar energy. When setting, the extra time flown costs battery without the compensating solar gain — making straight-line flight optimal in the evening.
