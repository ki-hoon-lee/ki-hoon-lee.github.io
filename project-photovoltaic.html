Two Projects, One Question: How Do You Best Optimise a Solar Farm?

This pair of Jupyter notebook projects — both drawing on real Typical Meteorological Year (TMY) weather data — investigates solar PV optimisation from two different angles. The three cities chosen — Bath (UK), Seoul (South Korea), and Berkeley (USA) — are the three cities Ki-Hoon has lived in, making the analysis personally grounded as well as technically rigorous.

Project 1 asks: how should excess heat in PV panels be managed — harvest it with thermoelectric generators (TEGs), or remove it with active cooling? Project 2 asks: given a fixed land area or a fixed budget, how do you maximise energy output — and how does solar compare to wind?

TEGs vs Active Cooling

Balancing Heat: Thermoelectric Generators vs Active Cooling for Solar PV

PV panels lose efficiency as they heat up. This project simulates two strategies for handling that excess heat across three cities and three seasonal days — Winter Solstice (Dec 21), Equinox (Mar 20), and Summer Solstice (Jun 21) — and determines which delivers greater net energy benefit.

TEG Simulation Model

TEGs mounted on PV panel rear surfaces harvest thermal energy via the Seebeck effect. Their efficiency and power output were modelled using magnetic torque dynamics and thermodynamic equations:

TEG Efficiency (Carnot + Figure of Merit)
η = ((T_H − T_C) / T_H) × (√(1 + ZT̄) − 1) / (√(1 + ZT̄) + T_C/T_H)
T_H — PV cell temperature (K)  |  T_C — ambient air temperature (K)
Z = 0.0035 — figure of merit (Bi₂Te₃-based commercial materials)
h = 10 W/m²·K — heat transfer coefficient
P_total = η × A_panel × h × ΔT × N_strings × N_modules

Active Cooling Model

The cooling system maintains panels at 25°C (STC) — the standard test condition for optimal PV efficiency. Energy cost is modelled using a heat transfer coefficient and a Coefficient of Performance (COP):

Cooling Energy Cost
P_cool = (h_cool × A_panel × (T_cell − 25°C)) / COP
h_cool = 15 W/m²·K (realistic) or 5 W/m²·K (best-case)
COP = 3.0 (realistic) or 6–12 (theoretical best-case scenarios)

Key Findings

TEGs win under realistic conditions

Under realistic parameters, TEGs outperform active cooling in every city and every season. The energy consumed by cooling systems consistently exceeds the PV efficiency gains — net cooling gain is negative across all cases.

Seoul delivers best TEG performance

Seoul achieves the highest TEG output thanks to strong temperature gradients combined with solid irradiance. On equinox and summer days, TEGs deliver up to 3–4% of total PV system output.

Cooling only viable at theoretical limits

At h=1 W/m²·K and COP=12 — well beyond current technology — cooling becomes competitive during summer solstice in Berkeley and Seoul. Real-world systems would still favour TEGs.

Bath: TEGs offer passive resilience

Bath's cool, cloudy climate means minimal cooling benefit. TEGs offer a low-maintenance, passive strategy to recover a small but consistent fraction of waste heat — the only viable thermal management approach for low-irradiance climates.

Land, Budget & Wind Comparison

Power per Dollar: Is Solar Better Than Wind?

Given the same 200 × 200 m land area and $1,000,000 budget, which renewable strategy yields the best return — a dense solar farm, or a wind turbine installation? This project optimises GCR for land-constrained solar, then compares solar vs wind head-to-head on energy output, revenue, and break-even time.

Section 1 — Land-Constrained: GCR Optimisation

Ground Coverage Ratio (GCR) defines how densely panels are spaced. Higher GCR fits more panels but increases inter-row shading. Lower GCR reduces shading but wastes land. The relationship is:

GCR Layout
Row Pitch = Module Width / GCR  →  Rows = ⌊200 / Row Pitch⌋
At GCR = 0.5: Row Pitch = 1.984 m → 100 rows × 121 modules = 12,100 modules
Module cost: $80/unit → 12,500 affordable (land constraint dominates at GCR 0.5)

Simulations across GCR 0.3–1.0 showed that total energy output initially rises with GCR (more panels) but plateaus due to shading losses and diminishing returns. Berkeley showed the highest energy return per dollar at all GCR levels.

Section 2 — Budget-Constrained: Solar vs Wind

With a fixed $1M budget at GCR 0.5, annual energy output and revenue were simulated for both PV and wind installations. Electricity prices used: Bath $0.35/kWh, Berkeley $0.26/kWh, Seoul $0.13/kWh.

The Surprising Winner: One Large Wind Turbine in Bath

Deploying a single 1.5 MW, 80m-diameter turbine (~$1M) in Bath outperformed the PV farm — breaking even by mid-August and generating approximately $600k profit by year end. Bath's strong wind resource, combined with the highest electricity price of the three cities, made this the standout result of the entire study.

By contrast, 16 small 10 kW turbines (5D spacing, same budget) generated significantly less revenue — confirming that turbine scale and hub height are critical variables, not just count.
